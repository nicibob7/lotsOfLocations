Uruguay's History

Early 20th century

  • The first half of the 20th century saw Uruguay as one of the most prosperous nations in Latin America, known for its political stability, social welfare programs, and progressive policies.
  • In 1903, the Colorado Party and the National Party established a bipartisan system that dominated Uruguayan politics for decades.

Mid-20th century

  • In the mid-20th century, Uruguay faced some political challenges, including military interventions and economic fluctuations.
  • In the 1950s and 1960s, the country experienced a period of economic growth and industrialization, known as the "Uruguayan miracle."
  • However, political repression and human rights violations increased during the 1960s and 1970s as Uruguay faced the rise of leftist guerrilla movements and growing tensions.

Late 20th century

  • In 1973, a military coup took place, leading to the establishment of a military dictatorship. This period, known as the "Dirty War," involved widespread human rights abuses, including torture and forced disappearances.
  • The military dictatorship ruled until 1985 when Uruguay transitioned back to democracy, marking the beginning of a new era of political freedom and civil liberties.
  • In 1989, the left-leaning Frente Amplio (Broad Front) coalition was formed, becoming a major political force in Uruguay.

21st century

  • In the early 2000s, Uruguay faced economic challenges, including a severe financial crisis. However, the country implemented various reforms that contributed to its recovery.
  • During the 2010s, Uruguay continued to enjoy a relatively stable political climate and social progress, including the legalization of same-sex marriage (2013) and the decriminalization of abortion (2012).
  • The country also pursued a progressive drug policy, legalizing the production, sale, and consumption of marijuana in 2013, becoming the first country in the world to do so.
  • In 2019, Luis Lacalle Pou, a member of the center-right National Party, won the presidential election, ending 15 years of left-wing rule under the Frente Amplio.
